Communication changed vastly during the early growth of the Internet. The availability of home computers and dial up connections allowed anyone, anywhere to publish and share information freely. Rather than being well-connected to restricted outlets such as newspapers, publishers, and TV networks, new age platforms allowed the average Joe to share anything with the rest of the connected world.

Platforms like YouTube gave big TV networks a run for their money by allowing anyone with a camera to simply point, shoot, upload, and share to a potential audience of millions. Facebook and other social media platforms allowed people to connect and form social networks like never before. Need to keep in touch with that old friend from your hometown? Music consumption in the early internet changed the way we listened to music forever. Giants like IBM spent millions of dollars in research with various record companies to see if consumers would burn CDs given the technology, they claimed that this would not be the case. However, with increasing record pricing, thrifty and clever consumers started to burn CDs onto their computers and save them as the newly formed and standardized digital music format, MP3. The advent of saving a single song as a MP3 file lead to early internet adopters rushing to file sharing programs such as Napster, to share their favorite singles and burn their own mixtapes.
